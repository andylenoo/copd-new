Common Symptoms

1. Persistent cough with mucus

A chronic cough that produces phlegm is often an early sign of COPD, caused by airway inflammation and mucus buildup.

2. Shortness of breath, especially with activity

Difficulty breathing during physical exertion occurs as lung function declines and airflow becomes restricted.

3. Wheezing

A high-pitched whistling sound while breathing, caused by narrowed or obstructed airways due to inflammation.

4. Chest tightness

A feeling of pressure or heaviness in the chest, often resulting from trapped air and reduced airflow in the lungs.

5. Frequent respiratory infections

People with COPD are more prone to infections like bronchitis or pneumonia, which worsen symptoms and lung damage.

For the possible clinical and subsequent laboratory diagnosis of Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD) and determination of its severity; healthcare providers typically use a combination of tools and criteria.

What You Can Do Now

  • A- Reflect on your daily symptoms (feel breathless, cough, or feel fatigued)
  • B- Count how many exacerbations or hospital visits you’ve had in the past year.
  • C- Get a spirometry test

A1. COPD Test

Some quick 8-question tool to measure the impact of COPD on your health. Each question is scored from 0 (no impact) to 5 (maximum impact), for a total symptom score (SS) out of 40.

Sample (COPD) questions (rate 0 to 5):

  1. I never cough – I cough all the time
  2. I have no phlegm – My chest is full of phlegm
  3. I am not breathless – I am very breathless
  4. I sleep soundly – I don’t sleep well
  5. I have lots of energy – I have no energy

Interpretation of total score:

  1. 0–10: Low impact
  2. 11–20: Medium impact
  3. 21–30: High impact
  4. 31–40: Very high impact

A2. Dyspnea Scale

Focuses specifically on breathlessness (Dyspnea Scale).

Scale:

  1. 1: Breathless when hurrying or walking up a hill
  2. 2: Walks slower than people of the same age or stops for breath when walking
  3. 3: Stops for breath after walking about 100 meters or a few minutes
  4. 4: Too breathless to leave the house or breathless when dressing

B. Exacerbation History

  1. Frequent or severe exacerbations (flare-ups) indicate more serious disease.
  2. ≥2 per year, or ≥1 leading to hospitalization is considered high risk.

To group your disease:

Combine your symptom scores (SS) with your history of flare-ups (exacerbations).

C. Get a Spirometry Test (Lung Function Test)

The FEV₁/ FVC and FEV1 (Forced Expiratory Volume in 1 second) is key to determining COPD severity.

COPD Stages (Based on FEV1/ FVC < 70% and FEV₁ % predicted):

COPD Stage FEV Criteria
Stage 1 (Mild) FEV ≥ 80% predicted
Stage 2 (Moderate) 50% ≤ FEV < 80%
Stage 3 (Severe) 30% ≤ FEV < 50%
Stage 4 (Very Severe) FEV < 30%

To stage your COPD: Combine symptom burden, exacerbation risk, and spirometry.

Laboratory and Diagnostic Tests for COPD

Below are the primary laboratory and diagnostic tests used to assess and manage COPD.

1. Pulmonary Function Tests (PFTs)

  1. Spirometry (most important test)
  2. Lung Volume Measurement (optional)
  3. Diffusing Capacity of the Lung for Carbon Monoxide (DLCO)

2. Pulse Oximetry (SpO2)

3. Arterial Blood Gases (ABG)

Used in advanced COPD or when hypoxia/hypercapnia is suspected.

  1. Indicated if:
    • FEV₁ < 40% predicted
    • Oxygen saturation < 92%

4. Chest Imaging

Used to support diagnosis and rule out other conditions:

  1. Chest X-ray
  2. Thorax CT (TCT) with axial, sagittal, and coronal sections

5. Alpha-1 Antitrypsin Level (Optional Blood Test)

6. Sputum Culture (if productive cough)

Don’t Ignore These COPD Symptoms — Get Evaluated Urgently

Early diagnosis of COPD is crucial because it slows disease progression, improves quality of life, reduces flare-ups, supports smoking cessation, prevents complications, and enables more effective, less costly treatment.

1. Sudden chest pain or shortness of breath

May indicate a pneumothorax (collapsed lung), which requires immediate medical attention to prevent further respiratory distress.

2. Fever or increased cough

Could be a sign of a respiratory infection such as pneumonia or bronchitis, especially concerning if symptoms worsen rapidly.

3. Increased mucus or blood in sputum

May suggest an infection, inflammation, or bleeding in the lungs or airways, and should be evaluated promptly.

4. Drop in oxygen levels

Indicates impaired lung function or oxygen exchange, potentially leading to hypoxia; urgent assessment and intervention are needed.

If you are experiencing one or more of the symptoms above, you should take urgent action and have the necessary examinations.
