Treatments of COPD

COPD is a progressive but treatable disease. The treatment of COPD (Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease) depends on the severity of the disease, symptoms, and history of exacerbations. The goals are to reduce symptoms, improve quality of life, prevent exacerbations, and slow disease progression.

Lifestyle changes (especially quitting smoking), medications, and sometimes oxygen therapy COPD patients can maintain a good quality of life and reduce the risk of complications.

1. Lifestyle and Non-Pharmacologic Management

Smoking Cessation (most critical)

  • The only intervention proven to slow disease progression
  • Offer nicotine replacement therapy, counseling, or medications like varenicline or bupropion

    • Pulmonary Rehabilitation

    • Exercise training, nutrition advice, and education
    • Especially helpful in moderate to severe COPD
 2. Pharmacologic Treatment
  • SABA (Short-Acting Beta Agonist): albuterol
  • SAMA (Short-Acting Muscarinic Antagonist): ipratropium
  • LABA (Long-Acting Beta Agonist): salmeterol, formoterol
  • LAMA (Long-Acting Muscarinic Antagonist): tiotropium, umeclidinium
  • ICS (Inhaled Corticosteroids): fluticasone, budesonide (used in select patients)

Triple Therapy (LABA + LAMA + ICS)

  • For severe COPD with frequent exacerbations
 3. Supportive Treatment
  • Oxygen therapy if PaO₂ ≤ 55 mmHg or O₂ sat ≤ 88%
  • Noninvasive ventilation (BiPAP) in hypercapnic respiratory failure
4. Vaccinations
  • Influenza (yearly)
  • Pneumococcal vaccines (PCV20 or PCV15 + PPSV23)
  • COVID-19
  • Tdap (for pertussis protection)
 5. Management of Exacerbations
  • Increased bronchodilators
  • Oral corticosteroids
  • Short-term antibiotics
  • Hospitalization if severe
 6. Surgical Options
  • Lung volume reduction surgery
  • Bullectomy
  • Lung transplant (in severe cases)
